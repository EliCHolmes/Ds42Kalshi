Kalshi Political Trade Analysis

How Big Money Moves in Political Prediction Markets

Calvin, Eli, Angela & Yash  |  DS4200 Spring 2026  |  Northeastern University

Introduction

Kalshi is a federally regulated exchange that allows people to trade on the outcome of real-world events—interest rate decisions, government shutdowns, and political elections. Unlike traditional polling, Kalshi functions as a prediction market where contract prices reflect the aggregate probability the market assigns to an event, providing a real-time look at public sentiment.

This data is particularly compelling because it is incentive-compatible: traders put real capital behind their predictions, which often makes these markets more responsive to breaking news than traditional data sources. By analyzing large-scale trade data from the Kalshi API, we move beyond surface-level percentages to examine the conviction, timing, and volume behind high-stakes political participants.

Our analysis focuses on a 30-day window (January 19 – February 18, 2026) of political trades valued at $2,000 or more, capturing the period around key events including the presidential inauguration, government funding deadlines, and cabinet nomination battles.

Analysis Tasks

We set out to answer three guiding questions through our visualizations:

  1. When does capital concentrate? Do traders spread bets evenly over time, or does volume spike around specific political events and deadlines?
  2. Where does conviction show up? Are the largest trades placed on uncertain 50/50 outcomes, or do big-money participants concentrate at extreme probabilities?
  3. How do different political topics trade differently? Do government shutdown markets, elections, and international affairs show structurally different patterns in timing, volume, and conviction?

Data Description

Source: Kalshi REST API (api.elections.kalshi.com), collected via a custom Python pipeline that paginates through all political trades globally and filters client-side.

Size: 7,646 trades across 559 unique markets, with 19 features per record after enrichment.

The dataset contains a healthy mix of categorical features (7 market topics, yes/no sides, 559 tickers) and continuous features (trade values ranging from $2,000 to over $40,000, implied probabilities from 1% to 99%). All trades occurred on the Kalshi exchange and represent real financial positions.

Data Suitability & Limitations

This dataset is well-suited for our analysis because it captures real financial commitments rather than survey responses or polling data—each observation represents actual capital at risk. The 30-day window around the presidential inauguration and government funding deadlines provides a politically dense period with multiple concurrent events, enabling cross-topic comparison. However, several limitations should be noted: the Kalshi API does not expose trader identity, so we cannot distinguish retail participants from institutional ones or track repeat behavior. Our $2,000 minimum filter excludes the long tail of smaller trades, which may exhibit different timing and topic patterns. The dataset also lacks order-book depth (bid/ask spread data), which would help separate liquidity-driven trades from conviction-driven ones. Finally, the 30-day window may not generalize to calmer political periods—our findings are most applicable to high-activity windows around major deadlines and transitions.

Summary & Conclusions

Through eight visualizations spanning macro overviews to granular deep-dives, we addressed our three guiding questions and identified key patterns in how capital flows through political prediction markets:

  1. Deadline-driven capital concentration: Political prediction markets see dramatic volume spikes as binary events approach their resolution dates. Government shutdown deadlines produced the largest single-day volumes in our dataset, suggesting that traders wait for maximum information before committing large positions.
  2. Conviction at the extremes: The largest trades consistently cluster at very high (>90%) or very low (<10%) implied probabilities, not in the uncertain middle. Big-money participants appear to trade on strong conviction rather than speculating on 50/50 outcomes—a pattern consistent with informed, rational capital allocation.
  3. Topic-specific trading behaviors: Different political categories exhibit fundamentally different trading patterns. Government Shutdown markets show steady accumulation toward deadlines, while International Affairs markets are driven by unpredictable news bursts. Understanding these structural differences is essential for interpreting prediction market signals as political indicators.

Limitations

Our analysis has several important caveats. The $2,000 minimum trade filter biases our sample toward high-conviction or well-capitalized traders, so these patterns may not hold for the broader market. The 30-day window captures an unusually active political period (inauguration, shutdown deadlines); calmer periods likely show less pronounced volume spikes and weaker temporal patterns. Without trader identity data, we cannot determine whether volume spikes are driven by many participants or a few large actors repeatedly trading. Finally, correlation between volume and event timing does not establish causation—external factors like media coverage cycles may also drive these patterns.

Future Work

Future work could extend this analysis to longer time windows to test whether these patterns persist outside high-activity periods, incorporate real-time news event timestamps for causal analysis, compare Kalshi prices with traditional polling data to evaluate prediction market accuracy, or model trader behavior segmentation based on trade size and timing patterns.

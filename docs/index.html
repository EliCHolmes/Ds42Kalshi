Kalshi Political Trade Analysis

How Big Money Moves in Political Prediction Markets

Calvin, Eli, Angela & Yash  |  DS4200 Spring 2026  |  Northeastern University

Introduction

Polls ask people what they think. Kalshi asks them to bet money on it. That's a big difference. Kalshi is a federally regulated exchange where people buy and sell contracts on real-world events like government shutdowns, elections, and interest rate decisions. Each contract's price works like a probability: if "Will the government shut down?" trades at 72 cents, the market thinks there's a 72% chance it happens.

What makes this data worth studying is skin in the game. Traders aren't filling out a survey or answering a phone call. They're putting thousands of dollars behind their predictions, and that kind of financial commitment tends to produce sharper, faster signals than traditional polling does. We pulled trade-level data straight from Kalshi's API to look at what's actually happening beneath the headline percentages: how much money is moving, when it moves, and which political topics attract the biggest bets.

We focused on a 30-day window from January 19 to February 18, 2026. This period covers the presidential inauguration, two government funding deadlines, and a wave of cabinet nomination battles. Only trades of $2,000 or more made the cut, giving us a dataset of serious, high-conviction positions.

Analysis Tasks

Three questions drove our analysis:

  1. When does capital concentrate? Do traders spread bets evenly, or does money pile in right before deadlines hit?
  2. Where does conviction show up? Do the biggest trades land on toss-up 50/50 outcomes, or do large bettors wait until they're very confident one way or the other?
  3. How do different political topics trade differently? Government shutdowns, elections, international affairs: do they attract different kinds of betting patterns, or does money behave the same way regardless of the topic?

Data Description

Source: Kalshi REST API (api.elections.kalshi.com), collected via a custom Python pipeline that paginates through all political trades globally and filters client-side.

Size: 7,646 trades across 559 unique markets, with 19 features per record after enrichment.

FeatureTypeDescription
trade_idIdentifierUnique trade hash
tickerCategoricalMarket ticker symbol (e.g., KXGOVTSHUTDOWN-26FEB14)

The mix of feature types works well for visualization. We've got categorical variables like market topic (7 categories), taker side (yes/no), and 559 unique ticker symbols. On the continuous side, trade values range from $2,000 to over $40,000, and implied probabilities span 1% to 99%. Every record is a real trade on the Kalshi exchange with real money behind it.

Data Suitability & Limitations

This dataset works for our questions because every row represents actual capital at risk, not a survey response or poll answer. And the 30-day window we picked isn't random. It covers the inauguration, two government funding deadlines, and a string of cabinet fights, so there's enough political action to compare across topics.

But the data has gaps. Kalshi's API doesn't tell us who's trading, so we can't separate retail bettors from institutional players or track repeat behavior. Our $2,000 floor cuts out all the smaller trades, which might show very different patterns. We also don't have order-book data (bid/ask spreads), so we can't tell whether a trade happened because of strong conviction or just because liquidity was thin. And 30 days during a politically intense period might not look anything like 30 days during a quiet stretch.

Visualization 4: Implied Probability vs Trade Value Altair

Each dot is a trade. The x-axis is implied probability (how likely the market thinks the event is), y-axis is trade value on a log scale, and dot size shows contract count. Color encodes topic, using a categorical palette instead of a rainbow. Interaction: Drag to select a region and highlight those trades. Hover for details on any individual trade.

Takeaway: Big money doesn't bet on coin flips. The largest trades cluster near 0% and 100% implied probability, not in the uncertain middle. Traders with $10,000+ positions are either buying "Yes" on things they think are near-certain, or buying "No" cheaply on long shots to profit at scale. The 40-60% range is relatively empty of huge trades. Uncertainty, it turns out, scares off the big bettors.

Summary & Conclusions

Eight charts, three questions. Here's what we found:

  1. Money waits for deadlines. Traders don't spread their capital evenly across the month. They sit on their hands until a deadline gets close, then pile in. January 30 alone saw $13.4 million in political trades as the government funding deadline approached. The pattern is consistent: the closer the binary outcome, the more money shows up.
  2. Big bettors want near-certainties, not coin flips. The largest trades in our dataset cluster at implied probabilities above 90% or below 10%. The 40-60% range, where outcomes are genuinely uncertain, is almost empty of huge positions. People putting $10,000+ on the line aren't gambling on toss-ups. They're betting on things they think they already know the answer to.
  3. Not all political topics trade the same way. Shutdown markets build gradually toward deadlines with volume and probability climbing in sync. International affairs markets spike unpredictably when news breaks, with no buildup pattern at all. If you're trying to use prediction markets as political indicators, the topic matters as much as the price.

Limitations

A few caveats worth flagging. Our $2,000 floor means we're only looking at high-conviction traders, and smaller bettors might show completely different patterns. We also can't tell if a volume spike comes from 500 different people or one whale placing 500 trades. The 30-day window we picked was politically intense by any standard, and a quieter month would probably look very different. And of course, just because volume spikes on the same day as a political event doesn't prove the event caused the spike. Media cycles and herd behavior could be doing some of the work too.

Future Work

A longer time window would help test whether these patterns hold outside high-activity periods. Matching trades to timestamped news events could move us from correlation to something closer to causation. And comparing Kalshi prices against traditional polling data would tell us whether prediction markets are actually better at forecasting, or just faster at reacting.

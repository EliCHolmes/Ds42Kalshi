========================================================================
VISUALIZATION 1: Daily Political Trade Volume Over Time (Altair)
========================================================================

Design idea:
We went with an area chart because the filled shape draws your eye to the
peaks and valleys better than a plain line does. You can immediately see
which days had huge volume spikes. The monotone interpolation smooths out
day-to-day noise but keeps the actual data points intact.

Key design choices:
- Dashed vertical lines mark real political events (inauguration, funding
  deadlines). Without those annotations, the chart just shows "volume went
  up." With them, you can see why it went up.
- Two-panel brush+detail layout. The top panel shows the full month, and
  you can drag to select a date range. The bottom panel then zooms into
  that window so you don't lose the big picture.
- Single-hue blue. One data series doesn't need multiple colors.

Alternatives we rejected:
- Bar chart (one bar per day): bars create gaps that break the visual flow
  and make it harder to see the overall trend.
- Multi-line by topic: way too many overlapping lines for an overview chart.
  We handle topic breakdown in Viz 6 instead.

Professor feedback we incorporated:
- "Add in annotations—what happens on that day?" → Added vertical rules
  with event labels for Inauguration Day, funding deadlines.
- "Introduce interactive plot" → Built the brush selection for date-range
  zoom.

VISUALIZATION 2: Top 12 Markets by Volume, Yes/No Stacked (Altair)
========================================================================

Design idea:
Horizontal stacked bars. The horizontal layout is better because market
titles are long and they read naturally on the y-axis. Stacking Yes and No
directly shows how volume splits between the two sides.

Key design choices:
- Blue (#4477AA) for Yes, coral (#EE6677) for No. We tested these with the
  Coblis CVD simulator and they're distinguishable for all common forms of
  color vision deficiency.
- Sorted by total volume, highest at top, so the biggest markets are the
  first thing you see.
- Human-readable titles, not raw ticker codes like KXGOVSHUT-26JAN31.
- Click the legend to isolate Yes or No. This is the key interaction
  because it shows you which markets had genuine two-sided disagreement
  vs. one-sided consensus.

Alternatives we rejected:
- Grouped (side-by-side) bars: harder to compare totals across markets.
  Stacking keeps the total comparison clean.
- Pie chart for Yes/No split: pie charts are bad for precise comparison
  and go against what we learned in DS4200.

Professor feedback we incorporated:
- "Top 12 Markets—combine yes and no (stack)" → Done.
- "Look into type of events" → Market titles show event types clearly.

VISUALIZATION 3: Trade Size Distribution by Market Topic (Altair)
========================================================================

Design idea:
Faceted histograms. Each topic gets its own panel so distributions don't
overlap and you can compare shapes side by side. Independent y-axes per
facet because the topics have very different trade counts, and we didn't
want smaller categories to get squished.

Key design choices:
- Tol colorblind-safe palette (#4477AA, #EE6677, #228833, #CCBB44). No
  adjacent colors are confusable under common CVD.
- 30 bins. Enough detail to see the shape of the distribution without
  making the bars too thin to read in the $2,000–$40,000+ range.
- Only the four biggest topics shown. The smaller ones would just add
  clutter without adding insight.

Alternatives we rejected:
- Overlaid density plots: with 4+ categories they overlap too much and you
  lose the raw frequency counts.
- Violin plots: we considered them but histograms are more familiar to most
  people and they show actual counts, not smoothed density.

Professor feedback we incorporated:
- "Not red or green for colorblind" → Tol palette throughout.
- "Change to shape" → We applied shape encoding in Viz 5 (the D3 scatter)
  where it makes more sense. Histograms use color per topic instead.

VISUALIZATION 4: Implied Probability vs Trade Value (Altair)
========================================================================

Design idea:
Scatter plot with a log-scaled y-axis. We wanted to show the relationship
between how certain the market is (probability) and how much money is at
stake. The log scale is necessary because trade values range from $2,000
to $40,000+, and a linear scale compresses everything to the bottom.

Key design choices:
- Tol categorical palette for topic encoding, not a rainbow gradient.
  Color encodes topic, which is categorical, not continuous.
- Point size encodes contract count. That's a third data dimension without
  needing a third axis or 3D.
- Brush selection lets you isolate a region and see which topics dominate
  at different probability levels.

Alternatives we rejected:
- Heatmap/2D histogram: individual trade identity matters here because the
  tooltip reveals the specific market title for each dot.
- Linear y-axis: the heavy right skew compresses most points to the bottom
  and makes the chart unreadable.

Professor feedback we incorporated:
- "Volume by Implied—do not use rainbow, take color off" → No rainbow.
  Categorical topic colors from the Tol palette instead.

VISUALIZATION 5: Interactive Trade Timeline Explorer (D3)
========================================================================

Design idea:
Every individual trade plotted by time and value. This gives the most
granular view of when big trades happen. We built it in D3 for smooth
brush+zoom transitions.

Key design choices:
- Shape encoding for topic (circle, triangle, square, diamond, star, cross,
  wye). This means you can identify topics without relying on color alone.
  That matters for accessibility.
- Log-scaled y-axis, same reason as Viz 4.
- Dashed trend line (linear regression in log space) showing whether average
  trade size trended up or down over the month.
- Brush to zoom + double-click to reset. That's the main interaction.
- Semi-transparent points (opacity 0.55) to reduce overplotting. We've got
  7,646 dots on this chart so transparency is not optional.

Alternatives we rejected:
- Jittered strip plot: loses temporal precision, which is the whole point.
- Animated replay: sounds cool, adds complexity, doesn't help analysis.

Professor feedback we incorporated:
- "Scatterplot—change to shape" → Each topic gets its own shape.
- "Not red or green for colorblind" → Same Tol palette as every other chart.
- "Indication line to highlight trend" → Dashed regression line overlaid.

VISUALIZATION 6: Market Deep Dive – Price & Volume Over Time (D3)
========================================================================

Design idea:
Dual-axis chart. Blue bars for daily volume (left axis), red line for
average implied probability (right axis). Putting them together lets you
see whether conviction and capital are moving in sync or independently.
The dropdown lets viewers switch topics and compare patterns.

Key design choices:
- Bars vs. line plus distinct colors makes sure you never confuse the two
  metrics, even though they share the same x-axis.
- Dropdown selector, not radio buttons or tabs. Scales cleanly to 7 topics
  and it's a familiar UI pattern.
- Animated transitions on topic change so viewers can track what shifted.

Alternatives we rejected:
- Separate charts for volume and probability: you lose the ability to
  directly see correlation or lack thereof between the two metrics.
- Small multiples (one per topic): takes too much space and splits attention.
  The dropdown keeps focus on one topic at a time.

Professor feedback we incorporated:
- "Use one topic with related questions" → Dropdown scopes to one topic.
- "Maybe introduce interactive plot → allows viewer to choose the topic"
  → That's the dropdown.
- "What policies/events occurred on that day?" → Volume spikes next to
  probability shifts show the impact of events.

VISUALIZATION 7: Trade Activity Heatmap – Day of Week × Hour (D3)
========================================================================

Design idea:
Heatmap is the natural choice for a 2D categorical grid (day × hour) with
a single quantitative variable (volume). It reveals temporal trading patterns
that are invisible in the time series charts.

Key design choices:
- Sequential single-hue blues. Simplest effective color scale for one
  quantitative variable. No rainbow, no diverging scale because there's
  no meaningful midpoint in trade volume.
- Rounded corners on cells. Looks cleaner.
- Abbreviated day names (Mon, Tue...) and AM/PM hour labels because
  24-hour time is less intuitive for most viewers.
- Gradient legend with a labeled axis so you can look up exact values.

Alternatives we rejected:
- Calendar heatmap (GitHub-style): shows specific dates, not the
  day-of-week × hour pattern we're after.
- 3D surface plot: adds no information and goes against DS4200 principles
  about unnecessary 3D.

Professor feedback we incorporated:
- "No unnecessary 3D" → 2D heatmap.
- "Do not use rainbow" → Sequential blues only.

VISUALIZATION 8: Market Category Breakdown (D3)
========================================================================

Design idea:
Bar chart with a dropdown to switch between metrics (total volume, trade
count, average trade size). The whole point is to prevent the mistake of
drawing conclusions from a single number. A topic that leads in total volume
might rank low in average trade size, and that distinction matters.

Key design choices:
- Same Tol topic colors as every other chart. Consistency across the page.
- Animated bar transitions when you switch metrics so you can watch
  categories re-rank in real time.
- Value labels on top of each bar so you can read exact numbers without
  going back to the axis.
- Tooltip includes unique market count, which adds context that isn't
  visible in the bars themselves.

Alternatives we rejected:
- Treemap: our categories are flat, not hierarchical. And treemaps are
  harder to read precisely than bar charts.
- Stacked bar with all metrics: the three metrics have different units
  (dollars, count, dollars) and stacking them would be misleading.

Professor feedback we incorporated:
- "Look into countries, type of events" → International Affairs groups
  country-focused markets. Tooltip shows unique market counts per topic.
- "Thinking about color blindness" → Tol palette throughout.
